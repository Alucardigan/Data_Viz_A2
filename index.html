The Australian Real Estate Bubble

By Thejas Thekkekara Vinod

The Problem


Australia's real estate market has seen significant price increases, making housing less affordable for many. Affordability varies widely across states, with some regions facing more severe challenges. Rising property costs, along with slower wage growth, have made it harder for households to afford homes.

Affordability is measured by comparing median home prices with median household incomes, showing how much of a household's earnings are consumed by housing costs. The darker areas on the map represent regions where housing is less affordable, meaning that a greater proportion of income is required to buy a home.


The donut chart represents the different tenure types in Australia, showing what percentage of the population is renting, owning with a mortgage, or owning outright or on social housing.

This visualization highlights how Australians secure housing, giving insight into the proportion of the population relying on renting versus owning. It helps illustrate the broader context of housing affordability and how it impacts people's living arrangements.

It can be see that over the years, a higher percentage of the population are renting or have paying a mortage

Signs of a bubble


Economists often use different metrics to identify when a market has a bubble. The most common metric would be to compare the Housing Price Index (HPI) to Consumer Price Index (CPI) or Work Price Index(WPI)

HPI represents the housing affordability across Australia, CPI represents cost of goods/inflation rate while WPI represents the labour costs

The line chart represents these indexes over time, with HPI growing much faster than both CPI and WPI

The stress of a bubble can be measuered by looking at the percentage of income that is spent on housing by the population

The stacked bar chart represents the different quantiles of income that is spent on housing over the years.

The growth of the middle 2 quantiles(25%-50%) showcases a worrying trend of increasing income pressures

How did we get here?

One of the main reasons for the current housing bubble is the lack of construction of new housing.
Property has become a speculative asset that contributes to the Australian GDP which incentivises compounding of housing prices.
Changes to the Foreign Investment Review Board and COVID-19 have further contributed to slower housing building.
